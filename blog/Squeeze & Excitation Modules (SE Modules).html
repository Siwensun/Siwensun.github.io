Squeeze & Excitation Modules (SE Modules)

All my understanding is based on the below two papers:

  • Concurrent Spatial and Channel ‘Squeeze & Excitation’ in Fully Convolutional Networks
  • Squeeze-and-Excitation Networks

SE blocks have shown their power in both classification and segmentation tasks, and fortunately, they can be seamlessly integrated within any CNN model.

What is it?

A good picture is more than thousands of words.

From the architectural configuration above, the most straightforward impression we could get is the blocks with different colors within the same batch of images. A very important charasteristic of conventional layer is to share weights spatially, which actually hypothesize the local pattern as the whole spatial pattern. SE modules is design to improve the representation of spatial and channel information respectively. To make the channel and spatial information jointly improved, provided we only tune them by weighting respectively, it is natural to let them decided by each other. Its nomenclature tells us how it is designed:

 

"The SE block ‘squeezes’ along the spatial domain and ‘excites’ or reweights along the channels."

 

In fact, this quote only illustrates the principal of cSE, which is described as the image(a). Naturally, the principal of sSE module can be summarized as "the SE block 'squeezes' along the channel-wise domain and 'excite' spatially". More mathematically, the cSE module can be expressed as:

Why does it work?

The most primitive version of SE modules is only the channel SE modules and is applied in the classification tasks. Channel information is more important than spatial information in classifiation tasks because we tend to hypothesize that the local pattern could wholly represent the global pattern in most classification tasks, thus we tend to recalibrate the channel information which should vary importance among different channels. While in segmentation tasks, spatial information definitely varies between spatial blocks, so the sSE is required.

The reason why SE modules work is that they encode the interdependence relationship between channel-wise and spatial information via 'squeeze' and 'excitation' each other. From my perspective of view, SE module plays the role of narrowing the gap between the convolution layer and fully connection layer. To explain this, let's see how convolution works first.

 

 

The lower blue map is the input feature map and the upper green map is the output feature map, we could safely conclude that, in the short term, pixel b in the input feature map would not contribute to or influence on the pixel A in the output feature map because of the weight sharing policy of convolution. If we want to make pixel b impact pixel A, we should enlarge the size of convolution kernel, the most extreme case is the kernel size is the input map size, which is also can be seen as the fully connection. But we know apply fully connection everywhere means too heavy computation, cSE modules give non-local pixels a chance to join the local feature transformation despite of being in the form of 'coarse' tune. I think sSE modules could get the similar explanation and the only difference is that here, the channel information is impacting but the spatial information is impacted.

I don't know how the author gets the idea of 'squeeze & excitation', but for me, it seems to be natural to think from the angle of non-local feature extraction. Here I would like to share some other interpretations of this method with you: a short cut for non-local feature (spatially or channel-wise) to be involved into the local feature mapping, or attention mechanism to emphasize the really valuable channels or spatial regions.

 

How to use it?

It can be seamlessly intergrated into any convolution layer because it change nothing about dimension. Take U-unet as example, we could add SE modules to every basic encoder module or decoder module. A detailed code example will be posted in the next chapter.

        self.SCSE = SCSE(out_channels)
        
    def forward(self, x, e=None):
        x = F.interpolate(x, scale_factor=2, mode='bilinear', align_corners=True)
        if e is not None:
            x = torch.cat([x,e], 1)

        x = self.SCSE(x)
        
        return x

 

Ps:

You may concern about the complexity problem potentially brought about by SE modules because it introduces fully connected layer, but actually the increase of parameters is very limited, compared with the original amount, even though you add SE modules after every cnn layer. The reason is the dimension of , and only related to channel number and in normal, the channel number is almost nothing compared with that of the final fully connected layer parameters.
